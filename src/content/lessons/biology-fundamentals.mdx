## What actually makes a shark a shark?

Sharks belong to a group of fishes called **chondrichthyans**, which also includes rays and
chimaeras. The single most important feature they share is a skeleton made of **cartilage**
rather than bone — the same flexible material in the tip of your nose.

Three other features show up again and again across sharks:

- **Skin like sandpaper.** Shark skin is covered in tiny tooth-like scales called *dermal
  denticles* that reduce drag and protect the animal.
- **Many rows of replaceable teeth.** Sharks continually grow and shed teeth throughout life,
  so a worn or lost tooth is quickly replaced.
- **Powerful senses.** Beyond sight and smell, sharks detect tiny electrical fields produced by
  living animals using sensory pores called the *ampullae of Lorenzini*.

> **Common misconception:** "Sharks have bones like other fish."
> **The correction:** They do not. Sharks have **cartilage**, not bone. This is a defining
> trait of the whole group and one reason shark fossils are usually known from teeth and
> denticles rather than complete skeletons.

<summary>Going deeper: breathing, buoyancy, and reproduction</summary>

**Breathing.** Sharks pass water over their gills to extract oxygen. Some species can pump
water while resting on the seabed; others rely more on swimming to keep water flowing — but the
popular idea that *every* shark must swim constantly or die is an over-generalisation.

**Buoyancy.** Bony fish use a gas-filled swim bladder to stay afloat. Sharks instead rely
heavily on a large, oil-rich **liver**, which is less dense than water and provides lift.

**Reproduction.** Sharks show a remarkable range of strategies. Some lay **egg cases** (like
the Port Jackson shark's spiral capsule); others give **live birth**. This diversity is part of
why sharks are so interesting biologically — and why a single rule rarely fits them all.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Name one sense sharks have that humans lack.</summary>

Detection of **electrical fields** through the *ampullae of Lorenzini*. (Acute smell and the
lateral-line sense for detecting water movement are also strong answers.)
