## Sharing the water

Diving alongside a shark is, for most people, the moment fear gives way to fascination. The
goal of this pathway is to help you reach that moment **safely** and in a way that leaves the
animal — and the reef — undisturbed.

Different sharks call for different experience levels. Many species are encountered on ordinary
recreational dives:

- **Beginner-friendly:** docile, reef-resting species such as the **Port Jackson shark** are
  approachable on standard Open Water dives.
- **Iconic but gentle:** filter-feeding giants like the **whale shark** are met on guided
  snorkel and dive tours in specific seasons and locations.
- **Specialist encounters:** species such as the **great white** are observed under controlled,
  operator-led conditions and are not casual recreational dives.

## A simple ethics code

Responsible shark diving follows the same principles promoted by international standards such as
**Green Fins**: minimise your impact, and let wildlife stay wild.

1. **Look, don't touch.** Never touch, ride, chase, or corner a shark.
2. **Control your buoyancy.** Avoid kicking up sediment or contacting the reef.
3. **Don't feed or bait casually.** Leave any provisioning to permitted, regulated operators.
4. **Give space and an exit.** Let the animal move freely; never block its path.
5. **Choose responsible operators.** Favour dive centres that follow recognised environmental
   standards.

> **Common misconception:** "Touching or feeding a shark makes for a better encounter."
> **The correction:** It makes for a **worse** one — for the shark. Handling stresses the animal
> and unregulated feeding alters natural behaviour. The best encounters are calm, hands-off, and
> on the shark's terms.

<details>
<summary>Going deeper: reading shark behaviour</summary>

Calm sharks cruise smoothly and pay you little attention. Signs that an animal wants more space
include tightening circles, exaggerated swimming, or repeated close passes. The correct response
is always the same: **stay calm, stay vertical if possible, keep the animal in view, and
withdraw steadily**. A good operator briefs you on the specific species before you enter the
water — listen to that briefing.

</details>

## Quick check

<details>
<summary>What is the single most important rule of shark diving?</summary>

**Look, don't touch.** Keeping your hands off the animal protects it from stress and protects
you from provoking a defensive response.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Which species here is suitable for a newly certified diver?</summary>

The **Port Jackson shark** — a small, docile, reef-resting species commonly seen on standard
recreational dives in temperate Australia.

</details>
