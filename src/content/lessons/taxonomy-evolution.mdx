## Organising the diversity of sharks

There are several hundred species of living sharks, and scientists organise them into a
nested hierarchy: **order → family → genus → species**. The living sharks are usually grouped
into **eight orders**, each with its own distinctive body plan and history.

You can meet one flagship species from each order on this site:

- **Lamniformes** — the mackerel sharks, including the great white.
- **Orectolobiformes** — the carpet sharks, including the whale shark.
- **Carcharhiniformes** — the ground sharks, including the tiger shark.
- **Hexanchiformes** — the cow and frilled sharks, including the bluntnose sixgill.
- **Squaliformes** — the dogfish sharks, including the Greenland shark.
- **Squatiniformes** — the angelsharks.
- **Pristiophoriformes** — the sawsharks.
- **Heterodontiformes** — the bullhead sharks, including the Port Jackson shark.

> **Common misconception:** "Scientific names and classifications are fixed forever."
> **The correction:** Classification is a **living hypothesis**. As new anatomical and genetic
> evidence arrives, scientists revise where species sit. The tiger shark, for example, was long
> placed in the family Carcharhinidae and is now placed in its own family, **Galeocerdonidae**.

<details>
<summary>Going deeper: why classifications change</summary>

Early classifications relied mainly on **anatomy** — body shape, fin position, tooth form. Modern
work adds **molecular data**, comparing DNA across species to reconstruct evolutionary
relationships. Sometimes the two lines of evidence agree; sometimes molecular data reveal that
animals which *look* alike are not closely related, or vice versa.

When that happens, taxonomists may split a group into new families or move a species. This is
not scientists "getting it wrong" — it is the method working as intended, sharpening the family
tree as evidence accumulates. Authorities such as the World Register of Marine Species (WoRMS)
track these accepted changes.

</details>

## Quick check

<details>
<summary>How many orders of living sharks are usually recognised?</summary>

**Eight.** Each has a distinctive body plan, from the streamlined mackerel sharks to the
flattened angelsharks.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Why might a species be moved to a different family?</summary>

Because **new evidence** — especially genetic data — shows its relationships differently than
older, anatomy-only classifications did. The tiger shark's move to Galeocerdonidae is one
example.

</details>
