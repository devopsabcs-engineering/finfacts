## The ocean's most famous predator

The **white shark** (*Carcharodon carcharias*) is the largest predatory fish alive,
reaching lengths of around **6 metres**. Despite a fearsome reputation built largely
by film and headlines, it is a wide-ranging, slow-to-reproduce animal that is now
assessed as **Vulnerable** on the IUCN Red List.

White sharks are *regionally endothermic*: special blood-vessel networks let them keep
their swimming muscles, stomach, and brain warmer than the surrounding water. That extra
warmth helps power fast bursts of speed and lets them hunt in cool, food-rich seas.

<details>
<summary>Going deeper: what "regional endothermy" really means</summary>

Most fish are *ectotherms* — their body temperature simply matches the water. White
sharks and their relatives in the family **Lamnidae** use a heat-exchanger called a
*rete mirabile* ("wonderful net") to trap metabolic heat before it is lost at the gills.
This is **not** full warm-bloodedness like a mammal; it is a targeted warming of specific
tissues. It is one reason the same family includes some of the fastest sharks in the sea.

</details>

### Is it really a threat to people?

This is the misconception worth correcting head-on. It is easy to assume, from movies,
that white sharks actively hunt swimmers. **They do not.** White sharks are recorded in the
International Shark Attack File, but bites are rare, and most are brief, exploratory
contacts rather than feeding attempts. Far more sharks are killed by people each year than
the reverse — the conservation problem runs in the opposite direction to the popular story.
