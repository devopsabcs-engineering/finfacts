## The sea's great generalist

The **tiger shark** (*Galeocerdo cuvier*) is a large, wide-ranging predator named for the
dark bars that mark young animals. More than almost any other shark, it is defined by its
diet: tiger sharks eat an extraordinary range of prey, from fish and sea turtles to
seabirds and even floating debris.

A note on names: you may find the tiger shark listed in older books under the family
**Carcharhinidae** (the requiem sharks). Current taxonomic authorities place it in its own
family, **Galeocerdonidae** — a good example of how classification is revised as new
evidence accumulates.

<details>
<summary>Going deeper: why its teeth are special</summary>

The tiger shark has uniquely shaped, deeply notched and heavily serrated teeth that work
like a saw. This dentition lets it cut through hard materials — including the shells of sea
turtles — that most other sharks cannot tackle. The tooth shape is so distinctive that it
alone can identify the species.

</details>

### Reading the "attack" record honestly

The tiger shark appears often in shark-bite statistics, which can make it sound
indiscriminately dangerous. The more accurate reading is that it is a large, curious,
coastal generalist that overlaps with where people swim — and even so, bites are rare. The
species is assessed as **Near Threatened**, a reminder that the pressure on tiger sharks is
greater than the pressure they place on us.
