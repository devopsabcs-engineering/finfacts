## The gentle reef sleeper with two kinds of teeth

The **Port Jackson shark** (*Heterodontus portusjacksoni*) is a small, mild-mannered shark of
southern Australia's temperate reefs. With its blunt, pig-like head, bony brow ridges, and a
bold harness pattern, it looks quite unlike the streamlined predators people usually picture.

Its name in science tells you something about how it eats. *Heterodontus* means **"different
teeth"**: small, pointed teeth at the front for grabbing, and broad, flat plates at the back
for *crushing* hard-shelled prey like sea urchins and shellfish.

<details>
<summary>Going deeper: the spiral egg case</summary>

Port Jackson sharks are **egg-layers**. The female produces an unmistakable corkscrew- or
screw-shaped egg case and wedges it into rock crevices, where the spiral helps anchor it
against currents. Finding one of these spiral cases on a dive or a beach is a direct, tangible
sign of shark reproduction — and a reminder that not all sharks give live birth.

</details>

### A friendly face for shark education

Because it is calm, common, and easy to approach, the Port Jackson shark is one of the best
species for introducing people to real shark behaviour. It is assessed as **Least Concern**,
and a typical encounter — a small shark resting quietly in a reef gutter — does more to correct
shark fears than any statistic. It is a fitting bookend to a tour through the diversity of the
living shark orders.
