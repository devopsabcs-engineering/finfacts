## A living link to ancient sharks

The **bluntnose sixgill shark** (*Hexanchus griseus*) carries a feature that sets it apart
from most sharks alive today: **six gill slits** on each side instead of the usual five.
Together with its single, far-back dorsal fin, this body plan is regarded as *ancestral* —
closer to the pattern of very early sharks.

It is a creature of the deep. By day it usually stays far below the depths divers can
reach, which is the main reason people so rarely see one.

<details>
<summary>Going deeper: why gill-slit counts matter</summary>

The number of gill slits is a useful clue to a shark's evolutionary position. The vast
majority of modern sharks settled on five. The cow sharks (family **Hexanchidae**) retained
six or seven, a trait shared with fossil forms. Studying them helps scientists reconstruct
what early sharks may have looked like and how the modern five-gill body plan arose.

</details>

### Rare to see is not the same as dangerous

Because the sixgill lives so deep, it almost never crosses paths with swimmers or divers.
Its low place in shark-bite records reflects that separation of habitats, not aggression.
Like many slow-growing deepwater sharks, it is more threatened by fishing than it is a
threat to anyone — and is assessed as **Near Threatened**.
