## The shark with a saw for a snout

The **common sawshark** (*Pristiophorus cirratus*) carries one of the most unusual tools in
the shark world: a long, flat **rostrum** lined with teeth, with a pair of whisker-like
barbels halfway along it. The saw is not for slicing through large prey — it is a sensory and
disabling tool for finding small animals hidden in the seabed.

A frequent mix-up is worth heading off early: sawsharks are **not** sawfishes. They look
similar, but sawfishes are *rays*.

<details>
<summary>Going deeper: sawshark versus sawfish</summary>

Two features separate them cleanly. First, **gill position**: sawsharks have gill slits on
the *sides* of the head (a shark trait); sawfishes have them *underneath* (a ray trait).
Second, **barbels**: sawsharks have a pair of long barbels on the rostrum, which sawfishes
lack. So although both carry a toothed "saw", they sit in very different parts of the
chondrichthyan family tree.

</details>

### Small, bottom-dwelling, and harmless

The common sawshark is a modestly sized seabed hunter of southern Australian waters. It poses
no danger to people and is currently assessed as **Least Concern** — a useful counterpoint to
the more threatened species on this site, and a reminder that shark conservation status varies
enormously from one species to the next.
