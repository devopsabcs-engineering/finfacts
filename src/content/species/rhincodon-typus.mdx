## The biggest fish in the sea

The **whale shark** (*Rhincodon typus*) is the largest living fish, reaching lengths
on the order of **18 metres**. Its name captures the paradox of the animal: it is
*whale-sized* but it is unmistakably a **shark**, not a whale. Whales are air-breathing
mammals; the whale shark is a cold-blooded fish that breathes through gills.

For all its bulk, it eats some of the smallest food in the ocean. The whale shark is a
**filter feeder**, swimming with its broad mouth open and straining plankton and small
animals from the water.

<details>
<summary>Going deeper: filter feeding among sharks</summary>

Filter feeding evolved independently in only a handful of shark lineages — the whale
shark, the basking shark, and the megamouth shark. Each uses modified gill structures to
sieve tiny prey from large volumes of water. It is a striking example of *convergent
evolution*: very different sharks arriving at the same solution to the problem of eating
plankton at scale.

</details>

### Why "harmless giant" is the accurate label

It is tempting to assume that anything this large must be dangerous. The opposite is true:
the whale shark has no interest in people and no predatory behaviour toward them. The real
risks at encounter sites run the other way — boat strikes and disturbance threaten the
sharks, which is part of why the species is now assessed as **Endangered**.
