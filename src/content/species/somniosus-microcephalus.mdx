## Possibly the longest-lived vertebrate on Earth

The **Greenland shark** (*Somniosus microcephalus*) is a giant of the cold, dark North
Atlantic and Arctic. It is famous for one extraordinary claim: it may live for **centuries**.
Radiocarbon dating of tissue in the eye lens points to lifespans far longer than any other
backboned animal known to science.

It earns its life of slow time honestly — this is one of the slowest-swimming sharks of its
size, moving through frigid water where everything happens unhurriedly.

<details>
<summary>Going deeper: how do you age a shark that has no growth rings?</summary>

Most fish are aged by counting growth bands, like tree rings. The Greenland shark's tissues
do not preserve usable bands, so researchers turned to the **eye lens**, whose core forms
before birth and is never replaced. By measuring radiocarbon in that core — and linking it
to known historical changes in atmospheric carbon — scientists estimated ages on the order
of centuries. The method gives a *range*, not a single exact number, which is why the figure
is reported with substantial uncertainty.

</details>

### Why "exact age" claims deserve caution

You will see confident, specific ages quoted for individual Greenland sharks. Treat them
gently: the dating technique produces broad estimates with real margins of error. The honest
summary is that this species is *extraordinarily* long-lived — likely the longest-lived
vertebrate known — even though the precise number for any one animal remains uncertain. That
same slow life history is why it is assessed as **Vulnerable**.
