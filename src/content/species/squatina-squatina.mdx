## A shark that looks like a ray

The **angelshark** (*Squatina squatina*) breaks the mental picture most people hold of a
shark. Its body is flattened and disc-like, with wide "wing" fins, and it spends its days
buried in the seabed waiting to ambush prey. At a glance it looks like a ray — but it is a
true shark.

There is also an urgent conservation story here. Once widespread across the north-east
Atlantic and Mediterranean, the angelshark is now assessed as **Critically Endangered**,
having vanished from large parts of its former range.

<details>
<summary>Going deeper: shark or ray? How to tell</summary>

The quickest test is the **gill slits**. In sharks — including the angelshark — the gill
slits open on the *sides* of the body. In rays they open underneath. Angelsharks also have
their pectoral fins separate from the head, whereas in rays the pectoral fins fuse to the
head to form the "disc". So despite the flattened shape, the anatomy places *Squatina* firmly
among the sharks.

</details>

### A reminder that "scary" and "endangered" are different questions

The angelshark is harmless to people, yet it is one of the most threatened sharks in the
world. That contrast is the heart of shark conservation: how dangerous an animal *feels* tells
you almost nothing about how much trouble it is in. Slow-reproducing seabed sharks like this
one are especially vulnerable to bottom fishing.
